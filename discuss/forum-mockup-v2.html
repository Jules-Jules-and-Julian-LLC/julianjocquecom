quantum coherence timescales in warm biological systems

arxiv.org/abs/2501.09832
julian · 2025-02-23 · 12 replies
This preprint claims 600fs coherence lifetimes in FMO at room temperature. That number has been contentious since Engel 2007. Their methodology looks tighter than previous attempts but I have questions about the spectral density they used. Posting here to think through it.
julian · 2025-02-23 14:32
The Drude-Lorentz spectral density they chose gives a reorganization energy of ~35 cm-1. Compare with Adolphs and Renger (2006) who got 39 cm-1 from fluorescence line narrowing. Close but I think the discrepancy matters at these timescales.
reply
chromatic · 2025-02-23 15:01
Have you looked at the Cho group's 2D-ES data on this? They get shorter lifetimes with a different bath model.
reply
julian · 2025-02-23 15:18
Yeah, Cho uses an Ohmic spectral density with exponential cutoff which kills coherence faster. The choice of bath model basically determines your answer, which is the whole problem with this subfield. You can get whatever lifetime you want by picking the right spectral density.

what this place is

This is my reading room. I collect links to things I find interesting and think about them here, sometimes out loud. You're welcome to sit down and join the conversation.

I built this because the internet used to have places where you could think slowly about interesting things with strangers who cared about the same stuff. Most of those places are gone or have been swallowed by engagement metrics, rage bait, and infinite scroll. This is not that.

There is no algorithm. The front page shuffles daily so nothing decays and nothing trends. Old threads sit next to new ones. Everything here was interesting enough to post; nothing expires.

* * *

I am the sole curator. Every post and comment passes through me before it appears. This is not a democracy and not a platform. It is one person's judgment about what's worth reading and discussing. If that sounds authoritarian -- it is, in the same way a bookshelf is authoritarian. Someone has to choose what goes on it.

When I remove something, you'll see why. The reason is always visible. This is how the site teaches you what it values -- not through a rules page, but through the accumulation of editorial decisions. Read the removals. They're part of the content.

Comments that aren't bad but aren't great get collapsed. Still there, just quieter. Think of it as me tilting my head and saying "eh."

* * *
be interesting or be quiet
This is the only rule. "Interesting" means you engaged with the material, thought about it, and have something to add. Agreement is not interesting. Disagreement without substance is not interesting. A link to a related paper with two sentences about why it matters is interesting.
ascii only
No emoji, no unicode tricks, no formatting beyond line breaks. If you can't make your point in plain text, the problem is the point, not the medium.
withered technology
This site runs on SQLite, Java, HTML, and CSS. No JavaScript on the forum. No frameworks. No analytics. No cookies except one, mine. It will outlive whatever you're reading this on.
discuss on a real machine
You can read the forum on your phone. You cannot post from it. Thoughtful writing happens at a keyboard, not a touchscreen. The chat is different -- chat from wherever you want.
no identity required
Post as anon. Or pick a name and two colors. Your name renders as a gradient -- it won't be cryptographic proof of anything, but regulars become recognizable at a glance. Nobody needs to know who you are. What you say is enough.
* * *

If you want to talk in real time, there's a chat. It's casual, it's ephemeral-ish, and you can use your phone. Different space, different energy.

If you have a link worth sharing, submit it. Tell me why it's interesting. I'll read it. If I agree, it goes up. If I don't, it doesn't. Nothing personal.

-- julian
