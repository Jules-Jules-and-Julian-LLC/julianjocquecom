Special Report B: Service and Sacrifice - The Thomas Brothers in the Civil War

 

The story of the Thomas family in the mid-19th century is dominated by the American Civil War. The decision of at least two sons of George Slater Thomas to fight for the Union while living in a Confederate state was a profound act of political conviction that carried immense risk. Their pension files, referenced in the source material, offer a window into their service and the war's devastating, long-term consequences for them and their families.

 

James Alvin Thomas (c. 1827-1861): The Tragedy of Camp Disease

 

James Alvin Thomas enlisted in the Union Army, a testament to his family's loyalty. His military career, however, was tragically short. He died on October 9, 1861, at Crab Orchard, Lincoln County, Kentucky, not from a battle wound, but from measles [Page 6]. His fate was tragically common; during the Civil War, disease was a far deadlier enemy than combat, killing roughly two soldiers for every one killed on the battlefield. Camps were breeding grounds for illnesses like measles, dysentery, and typhoid, which swept through units of men who had little prior exposure and immunity.

The story of his service is preserved in the application file for a widow's pension submitted by his wife, Mary Ann Baldwin. The file, designated as Widow's Certificate WC178.061, would contain the documents she had to gather to prove her claim: affidavits from her husband's comrades testifying to his service and death, proof of their marriage, and documentation of her financial need as a widow left to raise her children alone.24 This file is more than a military record; it is a poignant social document detailing the war's impact on the home front and the struggles of the families left behind.

Henry Patton Thomas Sr., James Alvin's brother, also served the Union cause. Unlike his brother, he survived the war, but its effects lingered. Source Evaluation

 

Genealogical research relies on a hierarchy of sources. Primary sources, created at or near the time of an event by someone with direct knowledge (e.g., a birth certificate, a marriage license, a census record), are the most reliable. High-quality secondary sources, such as a detailed obituary compiled with information from the immediate family or a scholarly article, are also highly valuable. Tertiary sources, like online family trees or data aggregator sites (e.g., MyLife.com), can provide useful clues but must always be verified against primary or strong secondary sources. This report prioritizes evidence from the most reliable sources to ensure the highest degree of accuracy.
